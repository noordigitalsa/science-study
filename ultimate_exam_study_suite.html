1. Atoms, Bohr-Rutherford & Ions

Finding Atomic Particles
  • Protons = Atomic Number.
  • Electrons = Atomic Number (for a neutral atom). If charged: subtract positive charge, add negative charge (e.g., $As^{3-}$ has 33 + 3 = 36 electrons).
  • Neutrons = Mass Number (rounded) - Atomic Number.
Bohr-Rutherford Cheat Sheet
  • Draw nucleus in center indicating protons ($p^+$) and neutrons ($n^0$).
  • Draw electron orbits around it: Max of **2** on 1st shell, **8** on 2nd, and **8** on 3rd.
  • Ions: Metal atoms lose electrons to form positive cations. Non-metal atoms gain electrons to form negative anions. Draw brackets around ion with charge in top right corner.

2. Ionic, Polyatomic & Molecular Compounds

Ionic vs. Molecular/Covalent
  • Ionic (I): Metal + Non-metal. Electrons are *transferred*. Stay together by electrostatic attraction.
  • Polyatomic (P): Contains a group of tightly bound non-metals with an overall charge (e.g., $Mg_3(PO_4)_2$).
  • Molecular (M): Non-metal + Non-metal. Electrons are *shared* (covalent bond).
Nomenclature Quick Rules
  • Binary Ionic: Name metal first, then non-metal ending with "-ide" (e.g., $K_2O$ = Potassium oxide).
  • Multivalent: Metals that can form multiple ions use Roman numerals for charges (e.g., Iron (III) oxide = $Fe_2O_3$).
  • Molecular: Use Greek prefixes (mono, di, tri, tetra, penta, hexa) for numbers of atoms (e.g., $PBr_5$ = Phosphorus pentabromide).

3. Reactant Types & Product Prediction

Synthesis & Decomposition

Synthesis: Two elements combine to form one compound ($A + B \rightarrow AB$).

Decomposition: A compound breaks down into two elements ($AB \rightarrow A + B$).

Single & Double Displacement

Single Displacement: One element replaces another in a compound ($A + BC \rightarrow AC + B$).

Double Displacement: Cations in two compounds trade partners ($AB + CD \rightarrow AD + CB$).

Combustion & Neutralization

Combustion: Hydrocarbon + Oxygen gas produces Carbon dioxide and Water ($\text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$).

Neutralization: Acid + Base produces Ionic Salt + Water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$).

1. Mirrors, Lenses & S.A.L.T. Properties

Mirror Types Cheat Sheet
  • Plane Mirror: S = Same size, A = Upright (laterally inverted), L = Behind mirror, T = Virtual.
  • Concave Mirror (Converging): Bends light inwards. Makes things upright & magnified (when close) or upside-down (when far). Used in makeup mirrors, solar cookers.
  • Convex Mirror (Diverging): Bends light outwards. Always produces a *smaller, upright, virtual* image. Wide field of view (security/blind-spot mirrors).
Refraction Bending Rules
  • Entering **denser** medium ($n$ increases e.g. Air $\rightarrow$ Water): light **slows down** and bends **towards** the normal ($\theta_2 < \theta_1$).
  • Entering **less dense** medium ($n$ decreases e.g. Water $\rightarrow$ Air): light **speeds up** and bends **away from** the normal ($\theta_2 > \theta_1$).

2. Vision & Human Eye anatomy

Eye Conditions & Lens Correction
  • Myopia (Nearsighted): Can't see far objects. Focal point lands in front of retina. Corrected with a **Diverging (Concave)** lens.
  • Hyperopia (Farsighted): Can't see close objects. Focal point lands behind retina. Corrected with a **Converging (Convex)** lens.
  • Astigmatism: Blurred vision due to an irregularly shaped cornea.
Total Internal Reflection (TIR)
  • Occurs when light travels from high to low optical density ($n_1 > n_2$).
  • The angle of incidence ($\theta_1$) must be **greater than the critical angle** ($\theta_c$), causing the light to reflect entirely back into the medium instead of refracting. (E.g., fiber optics, sparkling diamonds).

1. Animal vs Plant Cells & Cell Cycle

Primary Differences
  • Plant Cells: Have a rigid outer **Cell Wall**, large central vacuole, and **Chloroplasts** for photosynthesis.
  • Animal Cells: Have small vacuoles, no cell wall/chloroplasts, and contain centrioles for division.
Mitosis Stages (P.M.A.T.)
  • Prophase: Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes. Nucleus membrane disappears.
  • Metaphase: Chromosomes line up straight down the middle (equator) of the cell.
  • Anaphase: Sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite ends of the cell.
  • Telophase: Nuclear membranes reform around two new sets of DNA, ready for Cytokinesis (division of cytoplasm).

2. Levels of Organization & Interdependence

Tissues & Organization Hierarchy

Hierarchy: Cells $\rightarrow$ Tissues $\rightarrow$ Organs $\rightarrow$ Organ Systems $\rightarrow$ Organism.

  • Epithelial Tissue: Protects surfaces (skin).
  • Connective Tissue: Supports structure (bone, blood).
  • Muscle Tissue: Controls movement (skeletal, cardiac, smooth).
  • Nervous Tissue: Transmits signals (brain, nerves).
Interdependence (Organ Systems working together)

Organ systems are connected. For instance, to maintain homeostasis during exercise:

  • Respiratory System pulls in oxygen gas $\text{O}_2$.
  • Circulatory System pumps the oxygenated blood to your muscle cells.
  • Muscular System consumes oxygen to produce kinetic work.
